Chapter 10. Summary

These chapters have covered quite a bit of ground in learning HTML, CSS and JavaScript. The focus has been on Responsive Web Design when creating web pages, in particular mobile-first responsive web design. For those of you interested in following ongoing discussions on web development, or even taking part in those discussions, you should consider the writings of Rachel Andrew, Jake Archibald, Dan Cederholm, Lin Clark, Brad Frost, Denise Jacobs, Jeremy Keith, Ethan Marcotte, Jen Simmons, Sara Soueidan, Luke Wroblewski, ... There is also a collection of useful books published by A Book Apart.

Bear in mind whenever you look at any source that things move quickly on the web, so articles that made sense a few years ago may have been superceded, especially with regards their use of HTML and CSS. New ideas such as the use of CSS Flexbox and Grid layouts will mean that things that were difficult a few years ago (and which resulted in workarounds) can now be done much more easily.

The focus of the module has been on front-end web development, particularly static web pages rather than dynamically-built pages. The basics of JavaScript have been covered. However, there is much more remaining for your future learning. For example, we have not covered server-side programming. Modules later in your degree programme will give you the opportunity to experience and practise this – see COM3420 Software Hut and COM3504 The Intelligent Web.

Epilogue: Transferable skills

In creating this website, I have:

  • Made decisions about what should be presented;
  • Made decisions about the ordering of information;
  • Decided what should be assumed and what should be explained – here, I gave more detail in the early chapters and less in later chapters, assuming you would have developed the ability to seek out relevant information as needed;
  • Linked to relaible information sources, in particular Mozilla's excellent resources – the aim here was to establish credibility;
  • Used appropriate language, taking care with spelling and grammar.

As you work through the modules in your degree programme, you will develop similar transferable skills for finding, understanding, filtering, organising and presenting information.
